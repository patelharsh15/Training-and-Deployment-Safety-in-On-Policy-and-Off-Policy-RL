Motivation & Problem Statement

RL algorithms like PPO and SAC achieve high cumulative rewards, but real-world deployment demands more:

  • Stability — consistent behavior across training runs
  • Safety — adherence to constraints, low failure rates
  • Robustness — recovery from unexpected perturbations
Why does this matter?
Autonomous vehicles, robotic arms, and industrial control systems require predictable, safe policies — not just high rewards.

PPO (On-Policy)

Clipped surrogate objective → conservative updates
Known for training stability

SAC (Off-Policy)

Maximum entropy framework → exploratory behavior
Known for sample efficiency

Background & Research Gap

Prior Work

  • Schulman et al. (2017) — PPO: stable policy updates via clipping
  • Haarnoja et al. (2018) — SAC: maximum entropy for robust exploration
  • Henderson et al. (2018) — RL reproducibility challenges
  • Engstrom et al. (2020) — Implementation details matter more than algorithms
  • Andrychowicz et al. (2021) — Large-scale on-policy RL study

The Gap

Prior comparisons focus on cumulative reward and sample efficiency.

Missing: Direct, controlled comparison of training stability, policy smoothness, constraint adherence, and deployment robustness under matched conditions.

We selected these references as the foundational papers for PPO, SAC, and the most rigorous empirical methodology studies — they establish the evaluation standards we build upon.

Research Questions

Primary Research Question

In which environmental conditions will PPO exhibit more stable and safer behavior compared to SAC, during both training and deployment, given matched compute budgets and evaluation protocols?

Secondary Research Question

What are the algorithmic mechanisms that cause the observed differences in stability and safety between PPO and SAC?

How we evaluate this: We track gradient norms, policy entropy evolution, and Q-value variance to link behavioral differences to on-policy vs. off-policy mechanics.

Evolution from proposal: We removed the speculative "20% reduction" claim and instead let empirical results speak. We also added measurable metrics for the secondary RQ.

Results — Deployment Robustness

Recovery Ratio After State Perturbation (1.0 = perfect)

Perturbation Protocol

  1. Run policy for 50 steps (measure baseline performance)
  2. Inject Gaussian noise (σ=0.1) into state observation
  3. Continue running and measure post-perturbation performance
  4. Recovery ratio = post/pre mean reward
Why this matters: In deployment, sensor noise, environmental changes, and sim-to-real gaps introduce unexpected state perturbations. A robust policy should recover quickly.

Results — Summary

All values: mean ± standard error across 10 seeds. Smoothness = lower is better. Recovery = closer to 1.0 is better.

Analysis & Insights

🔑 Key Insight

The on-policy vs. off-policy distinction manifests most clearly in training variance and action smoothness, not just in final reward.

Algorithmic Mechanisms

  • PPO's clip bounds the KL divergence between consecutive policies → smaller, more predictable updates
  • SAC's entropy bonus encourages exploration → diverse but potentially less smooth actions
  • Off-policy replay introduces distribution shift between training data and current policy → higher gradient variance

Gradient Norms: PPO vs SAC

Notable Observations

  • Environment dynamics complexity modulates the safety gap
  • Higher-dimensional action spaces may amplify smoothness differences
  • The safety-reward tradeoff is not symmetric between algorithms

Limitations & Future Work

Limitations

  • Environment scope: 3 environments — may not generalize to all continuous control tasks
  • Single architecture: MLP 2×256; transformer or recurrent policies may behave differently
  • No constrained RL: We don't modify algorithms to enforce constraints (e.g., CPO, PPO-Lagrangian)
  • Perturbation model: Simple Gaussian noise; real-world perturbations are more structured
  • Compute budget: 1M steps may be insufficient for some conditions to fully converge

Future Work

  • Constrained RL methods: Compare PPO-Lagrangian, CPO, WCSAC for explicit safety optimization
  • Sim-to-real transfer: Test policy smoothness metrics on physical robots
  • Domain randomization: How do perturbation recovery metrics change with training-time randomization?
  • More environments: Walker2d, Ant, and higher-dimensional manipulation tasks
  • Adaptive entropy tuning: Investigate SAC's auto-α impact on safety metrics

Conclusion

Research Question Revisited

In which conditions will PPO have more stable and safer behavior compared to SAC?

Main Findings:
  1. PPO shows lower training variance across seeds — more reproducible policies
  2. PPO produces smoother actions — safer for physical deployment
  3. SAC achieves competitive or higher rewards but with greater variability
  4. In safety-constrained environments, the reward-safety tradeoff differs between algorithms
  5. Algorithmic mechanisms (clipping vs. entropy) directly explain the observed behavioral differences
Take-Away Message

When deploying RL in safety-critical applications, algorithm choice should be guided by stability and safety metrics — not just cumulative reward. PPO's conservative updates offer a natural advantage for deployment safety.
